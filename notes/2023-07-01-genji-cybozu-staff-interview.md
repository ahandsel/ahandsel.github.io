English translation of my staff profile interview on the Cybozu recruitment site. The Japanese original is reproduced verbatim in [2023-07-01-genji-cybozu-staff-interview-ja.md](./2023-07-01-genji-cybozu-staff-interview-ja.md). Site navigation and footer links are omitted.

* Role at time of interview: Engineer Marketing / UX Writer
* Published: July 2023


## Headline

Why I, born and raised overseas, joined Cybozu in Japan to take on the world

Engineer Marketing, Genji (joined as a new graduate in 2019)


## Profile

Born in Alaska, USA. Raised in the San Francisco Bay Area, California. Graduated from the University of California, Santa Cruz. After an internship at the Kintone USA office in the summer of 2018, he joined Cybozu's Tokyo office as a new graduate in April 2019. Assigned to the Engineer Relations Department in the System Consulting Division, he devotes himself to creating content for English-speaking developers and to coordination with the Kintone USA office. In 2024, he is active as a UX writer in the New Business Division.


## Meeting Cybozu in San Francisco


### You were living overseas, so how did you go about your job search?

I wanted to try working in Japan, so I searched for jobs in the United States while also job hunting in Tokyo. I really did not want to work in a suit, so I chose companies that did not give me a disapproving look when I showed up to interviews in business casual (laughs). At Cybozu, no one said anything about it.


### Did you have any other criteria for choosing a company?

I majored in information management and technology in university, so I was thinking about industries and roles where I could put that to use. However, the Japanese companies exhibiting at the San Francisco career forum I attended were quite varied, including major automakers and railway companies, so I may have ended up looking broadly. The career forum is also where I first met Cybozu.


### Why did you become interested in Cybozu at the career forum?

Osamu (former Executive Vice President of Cybozu) was talking about Cybozu's culture and environment, and I felt it seemed like a company where people could work comfortably. What stayed with me was that he acknowledged the company's past mistakes, when it had become an exploitative workplace, and talked about its current efforts. That was clearly different from other Japanese companies. While other companies were promoting their name or talking about numbers like sales targets, I liked that Cybozu was telling a story.


### Was there anything from the talk about Cybozu that left an impression on you?

I was especially surprised to hear that many women work in the System Consulting Division. In technical roles, the gender ratio tends to lean toward men at many companies, so I felt that Cybozu was seriously committed to diversity and inclusion. Also, the booths of other companies basically had only Japanese people, so it was striking that at Cybozu I could meet colleagues of mixed heritage like myself.


### Why were you concerned about the gender ratio in technical roles, Genji?

From my experience as a student, I felt that discussions are easier to move forward constructively when the participants have a balanced mix of backgrounds. At a Japanese company, I would also be a minority, so I thought that a diverse environment where women can thrive would be easier for me to work in too. And simply put, I figured a company is more interesting when people with all kinds of backgrounds come together.


### What was the reason you ultimately decided to work in Tokyo?

The number one reason is that I disliked driving (laughs). The United States is a car-centered society, after all. The trains in Tokyo are wonderful. On a more serious note, I had spent more than 80% of my life in California, so I wanted to take on the challenge of a new environment. On top of that, I had an offer from Cybozu, and a big factor was that I felt I wanted to work at such a good company. If I had only received offers from Japanese companies that I was not really convinced by, I might have ended up working in the United States.


## Cybozu accepted me even though I could barely read Japanese


### Did you have any worries about joining Cybozu?

At the time I could not read Japanese, so I was worried about whether I could communicate well. But I figured I could manage somehow if I used text-to-speech tools and translation tools. Other than that, I was also worried about whether the things I had heard about Cybozu at the career forum and elsewhere were actually true. I think that what people say in those kinds of settings inevitably includes some exaggeration or marketing.


### After you actually joined, how did those worries change?

I have been working here for years now, and I feel that what I heard before joining was true. This is a story from right after I joined: I had barely used kanji in my life, so I could not read my own surname, "Fujimori" (藤森), which was written on the seating chart. When I told a member of the Human Resources Department about it, they rewrote it in romaji for me. It would not have been strange if they had told me, "At least learn to read your own name." But instead they immediately rewrote it for me, and that made me glad I had joined Cybozu.


### Are there other good things about Cybozu that you noticed after joining?

I was also happy that the IT rules are lenient and that the culture lets employees customize things freely. At Cybozu, if you tell the IT department what you want, they will provide tools tailored to each person. In both Japan and the United States, many companies tell you to use company-specified equipment or say that customization is not allowed. I already had many programs I had made for myself before joining, and not being able to customize my tools would have made communication and input in Japanese even harder, so this was a big help.


### Have you had any experiences that felt very "Cybozu-like" to you?

I had worked on the same team all the way up to my fifth year, and just as I was thinking I wanted to try something different, a new department happened to be created. There was an open call on our internal kintone for an English writer and content manager, so when I wrote "I want to do it," a meeting was set up right away.

I had thought you could not change departments so easily, but I was happy when they told me, "You can adjust your current work and spend that time on the new work." And when I had expected the preparation and procedures to take about half a year, my dual role started in just two months. I like my current job and want to keep doing it, but I also want to try new things. Being able to casually talk it over and change your work at times like that feels very Cybozu-like to me.


## The importance of not doing your work alone


### Did you request the assignment to the System Consulting Division yourself?

Yes. I heard that the System Consulting Division had work that involved coordinating with the USA office, so I requested to be assigned there. My request was granted, and I was assigned to the Kintone Developer Program team, where I was entrusted with creating developer-facing content in English.


### What do you find interesting about the work of creating developer-facing content?

I enjoy trying out all kinds of new tools, setting up APIs to build integrations, and casually experimenting while looking for content ideas. And when the content I make gets a "like" or a comment of thanks, it makes me really happy.

Developer-facing content is basically read when there is some kind of bug or mistake, right? So it often gets negative comments. But sometimes people write "Thank you." At times like that, I feel that the work I put effort into was useful to someone, and that makes the job rewarding.


### Is there anything you keep in mind when you work?

A sense of speed. I have the mindset that if I am going to do the same thing three times, I should automate it to make things easier, so I spend 20% of my work time reviewing and streamlining how the work is done. When I create documents and templates this way, I can hand them over when a new member joins. If everything were only in my head, the work would stop whenever I took time off, and the team would never be able to grow.

Rather than asking, "Why is Genji writing programs that are not directly related to the work?", my senior colleagues trust me and leave these automation and documentation efforts to me.


### What has been the most memorable part of your work so far?

Migrating the kintone developer community from Zendesk to Discourse. At first I was doing it alone, but it did not go well, and I learned all over again how important it is to work together with other members.

When you do it alone, you often fall into a pattern of assuming you are the one who made a mistake and blaming yourself. But when you have people to help, it is easier to find solutions even when you get stuck, with comments like "It is a simple mistake" or "The problem is on the product side, so we should contact support."

I realized that skills like calling for help and asking questions are surprisingly important. Saying "I do not know, so please teach me" is embarrassing, right? But because I experienced a situation where the work would stop unless I relied on the people around me, I have become able to ask for help more openly than before.


## Becoming someone who connects Japanese members with overseas offices


### What do you want to work on going forward?

Cybozu is in a strong position in Japan, but I want us to build a greater presence in the world as well. For that, I think the members and their way of thinking need to become global first, before the products become global. I want to use my strengths and take action so that we can strengthen the coordination between the Tokyo office and overseas offices, starting with the USA office.


### What do you think are Cybozu's current challenges?

We still operate in Japanese only. Almost only Japanese people can read Japanese. Its reach is overwhelmingly different from that of English. If you live in Japan the whole time, Japanese alone is enough, but the moment you step outside the country, Japanese does not work at all. I want the members inside the company to understand this more. You might think it is just a matter of language, but it is a fact that simply being able to use English as a standard within the company makes it easier to hire global talent.

That said, Cybozu does have a department that supports communication across different languages, such as translation within the company, and I can feel the energy to turn weaknesses into strengths. If that kind of support increases the number of members with even more diverse backgrounds, then information in various languages should come to be shared within teams.


### What kind of people do you want to join Cybozu, Genji?

People who have individuality and a desire to take on challenges. I think Cybozu has many people who love something deeply and have the energy to want to share it with others. One of my senior colleagues said, "Everyone at Cybozu is a secret geek," and that really stayed with me.

Also, being able to respect other people's individuality is important. Everyone has their own way when it comes to personality, manner of speaking, and way of working, and it is good to have people who can accept that in one another.


### If someone is unsure about choosing a company, what would you tell them?

The amount of time you spend at a company over your life is surprisingly long. There are many reasons for choosing a company, but I recommend choosing one where the way of working suits you and where you feel that you would be happy to spend a long time together with these people. On that point, I think Cybozu is built for each individual, and because it has no unnecessary rules, it is a company where it is easy to work as your true self.


## My time off

On my days off, I like to hike in the mountains and take walks along the coast. Life in the city is fun too, but I do think it is important to go to the forest or the sea to refresh. I also want to see more Japanese serow (kamoshika). I have been hiking in Japan for over four years now, but I have only seen them three times so far!
